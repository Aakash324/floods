What is Flood?

A Flood is an overflow of water that submerges land that is usually dry. Floods can result from various causes, including heavy rainfall, snowmelt, storm surges from hurricanes or other tropical storms, or the breaching of dams or levees. Floods can occur gradually over time or suddenly with little warning, and they can vary in scale from localized to regional or even global events. Floods can have significant impacts on communities, causing damage to property, infrastructure, and agriculture, as well as posing risks to human safety and health.

Causes of Floods:

Floods can arise from a multitude of causes, both natural and human-induced. One primary natural cause is intense or prolonged rainfall, especially in areas with inadequate drainage or in regions susceptible to monsoons or tropical cyclones. Melting snowpacks during spring can also contribute to flooding, particularly in mountainous regions. Storm surges, generated by strong winds from hurricanes or typhoons, can push seawater inland, leading to coastal flooding. Additionally, geological factors such as landslides or volcanic eruptions can obstruct river channels, causing water to overflow onto surrounding areas. Human activities, including deforestation, urbanization, and improper land use practices, can exacerbate flood risks by altering natural drainage patterns and reducing the land's ability to absorb water. Furthermore, the failure of infrastructure such as dams, levees, or flood defenses can result in catastrophic flooding events. Overall, floods are complex phenomena influenced by a combination of natural processes and human actions.

1. Heavy Rainfall.

Intense or prolonged rainfall, particularly in areas with poor drainage systems, can lead to excessive runoff and flooding.

2. Snowmelt.

Rapid melting of snowpacks, especially during warmer weather or sudden temperature changes, can overwhelm rivers and streams, causing them to burst their banks.

3. Storm Surges.

Hurricanes, typhoons, and tropical storms can generate powerful storm surges, pushing seawater inland and causing coastal flooding.

4. Flash Flooding

Sudden and intense rainfall, often associated with thunderstorms or cloudbursts, can lead to rapid-onset flash floods, especially in urban areas with impermeable surfaces.

5. River Overflow

Rivers can overflow their banks due to a combination of factors, including heavy rainfall, snowmelt, or blockages caused by debris, leading to riverine flooding.

6. Landslides

Slope failures or landslides, triggered by heavy rainfall, earthquakes, or human activities, can obstruct river channels or create temporary dams, resulting in flooding upstream and downstream.

7. Coastal Erosion

Rising sea levels, coastal erosion, and subsidence can increase the vulnerability of coastal areas to flooding, especially during high tides or storm events.

8. Human Factors

Deforestation, urbanization, improper land use practices, and the construction of dams or levees can alter natural drainage patterns, exacerbating flood risks and increasing the likelihood of flooding in certain areas.

5 Initiatives by the Government for Prevention & Management of Floods

In this article, we are going to know about the five major initiatives that are taken by Indian goevernment in order to prevention and management of flood.

1. National Flood Control Program (NFCP)


Established to address flood management across the country, the NFCP focuses on measures such as construction of embankments, drainage channels, and reservoirs to regulate river flows and minimize flood damage.


2. Flood Forecasting and Early Warning Systems. (FFEWS) by the Ministry of Power




The Central Water Commission (CWC) operates flood forecasting stations across major rivers in India to monitor water levels and issue timely warnings. These systems help to evacuate populations at risk and minimize casualties.


3. Integrated Flood Management Program (IFMP)



The traditional approach to floods focused on reactive practices to reduce exposure to flooding and susceptibility to flood damage, mainly through structural measures separating the river from its floodplain. In the course of time, these ad-hoc interventions proved to be only partially effective, shifting rather than mitigating flood risk. The acknowledgment of the necessity of a wider multi-disciplinary approach led to a paradigm shift from flood control to flood management. This indicates a proactive mentality recognizing that floods can never be fully constrained, but, at the same time, present positive (besides negative) aspects. The concept of flood management lies within the framework of the Integrated Water Resources Management. The latter promotes a coordinated management of water resources, in order to optimize the resultant economic and social welfare without compromising the sustainability of ecosystems. Integrated Flood Management (IFM) endorses this idea, aiming at maximizing the net benefits from flood plains, meanwhile minimizing loss of life. In fact, the concept of IFM rests upon the consideration of the river basin as a unique dynamic system: within it, interactions between land and water resources ensure that every single change affects the other components in a positive or negative way.

    Tips to follow during Flood

    During a flood, it's crucial to prioritize safety and take appropriate actions to protect yourself and your loved ones. Here's a list of tips to follow during the occurrence of a flood:

    1

    Monitor weather forecasts and official flood warnings through radio, television, or reliable online sources. Follow instructions from local authorities and emergency management agencies.

    3

    If authorities recommend evacuation, do so immediately. Follow designated evacuation routes and don't attempt to cross flooded areas on foot or by vehicle.

    5

    If evacuation isn't possible, move to the highest level of your home or a sturdy building. Avoid basements or lower floors. Bring essential supplies with you.

    7

    Stay away from floodwaters, as they may be contaminated or contain hidden hazards such as debris, fast-moving currents, or downed power lines. Do not attempt to walk, swim, or drive through flooded areas.

    9

    Switch off gas, electricity, and water supplies at the mains if it's safe to do so. Disconnect electrical appliances to prevent electrocution and fires.

    2

    Keep a battery-powered radio or mobile device handy to receive updates and instructions from authorities. Be prepared to follow evacuation orders or shelter-in-place directives.

    4

    If you're trapped and need rescue, signal for help by waving a flashlight, hanging a sheet out of a window, or using a whistle or horn to attract attention.

    6

    Move valuable items to higher levels of your home to protect them from flood damage. Store important documents and belongings in waterproof containers.

    8

    Avoid unnecessary travel and stay indoors until authorities declare it safe to venture outside. Be cautious of weakened structures, debris, and other hazards in the aftermath of a flood.

    10

    Look out for elderly or vulnerable neighbors who may need assistance during the flood. Offer help if it's safe to do so.
